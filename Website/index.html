SitS:AweSOMSense

Multi-modal Sensing and Analytics Framework for Modeling Belowground SOM in Salt Marsh Wetlands

  • NSF Funded Project

Project Overview

Abstract

Visible-near infrared (VNIR) and shortwave-infrared (SWIR) reflectance spectroscopy have been widely used for in situ and satellite-based predictions of various soil properties, including SOM and SOC. The underlying premise is that the exposed soil color and composition of vegetation species associated with changes in the SOM are detectable in the VNIR and SWIR bands. Once the spectral features have been calibrated for SOM prediction using machine learning techniques, multi- or hyperspectral remote sensing data can provide a rapid and cost-effective estimation of SOM. Satellites such as NASA’s Landsat-8, ESA’s Sentinel-2A/2B, and WorldView-3 and 4 have been used in studies to predict salt marsh soil properties using data analytics techniques Several studies have reported a non-linear relationship between specific-band spectral features in remote sensing data and SOM. Machine learning techniques such as Random Forest (RF) or artificial neural network (ANN) have been used to exploit the non-linear relationship to predict the spatial distribution of SOM in croplands. None of these studies, however, have explored machine learning-enabled predictive modeling of belowground SOM using a combination of field sensing data, soil core, drone data, and satellite data. There have been only a handful of field-based studies that reported the depth-distribution pattern of SOC in salt marshes. In addition, automated sensing of salt marsh SOM in the field in a high-frequency manner has not been explored

Real World Applications

Heat Exposure & Safety

The findings from this study will be extremely useful for understanding the heat exposure vulnerabilities of individual communities such as people living in poorly-planned neighborhoods, poor and elderly, city and municipal outdoor workers, construction workers, bus commuters, and mail delivery personnel. Furthermore, this study will lay the foundation for city/local government officials and business leaders to devise targeted and more efficacious heat hazard mitigation efforts such as increasing greenspace and developing better heat-safety policies for their workers.

IoT Based Framework

Network of Sensors

This research will build a scalable and robust smart-sensor-cloud framework for leveraging variety of human and vehicle-borne smart sensors (e.g., smartphones, environmental micro data loggers) in conjunction with traditional data sources (e.g., satellites and weather stations) for gathering, and analyzing accurate and fine-grained temperature information for urban areas as well as specific urban communities.
